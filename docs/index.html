Building AI products that users actually trust
Most AI pilots fail before a single user touches them. Not because the model is bad. Because nobody thought about what happens when it's wrong. I've watched teams spend months on accuracy benchmarks, then ship a product with zero indication of confidence level, no fallback flow, and no way for the user to course-correct. The model was 87% accurate. Users trusted it 0%. Here's what I've learned: Trust isn't built when your AI is right. It's built in how the AI handles being wrong. Show uncertainty explicitly. "I'm not confident about this — here's why" is more trustworthy than a clean answer that's quietly hallucinated. Users aren't fragile. They can handle nuance. Give users control points. Let them override, flag, or undo. The moment a user feels trapped by a decision the AI made, you've lost them. Probably permanently. Match the stakes to the UI. A low-risk recommendation can be casual. A high-stakes output — financial, medical, legal — needs friction. Make users pause before they act on it. The teams building trusted AI products aren't the ones with the best models. They're the ones who treat error states as core product features, not afterthoughts.
