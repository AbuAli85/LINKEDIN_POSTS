# LinkedIn posts — three variations

---

## Post 1 — The project nobody wanted

Two years ago I almost turned down a project nobody wanted.

It was a six-week data cleanup. Three teams had been arguing about the same set of spreadsheets for months, and my manager framed taking it as "a favor." I knew it was a punishment in a nicer outfit. I said yes anyway, mostly because I had run out of better ideas.

Inside those spreadsheets was every assumption the org had made about customers for the last four years. About half of it was wrong. The other half was right for reasons nobody could remember. To clean the data I had to talk to every team that touched it, and by week three I understood more about how the business actually worked than people who had been there ten years.

When I presented the cleanup, I also presented what the data was telling us. Two of the findings turned into projects. One of them turned into my next role.

I'm not going to tell you to take every bad assignment. Some really are punishments and they stay that way. But I started paying attention to a specific kind of boring work — the kind that sits at a seam between teams, where everybody wants it gone but nobody can throw it away. That work has a habit of paying you back.

If you're earlier in your career and waiting for the right project to find you, look at the one nobody else wants. Sometimes it's the most useful six weeks you'll have all year.

## Post 2 — The "focus" advice that broke my workflow

Unpopular take: the advice to "stop multitasking and focus on one thing" has done more damage to my work than it has helped.

I tried single-tasking for about a year. Pomodoro timer, phone in the other room, one browser tab. The full setup.

What I got wasn't deep work. It was a slow, ceremonial relationship with my own to-do list. Every task became a production. Switching felt like a moral failure. I started avoiding things that needed me to hold two ideas in my head at once, because they wouldn't fit the format.

Then I noticed something. My best thinking has never happened inside a focus block. It happens when I'm writing a doc and a Slack message lands that reframes the problem. When I'm in a meeting and an email I half-read three days ago suddenly makes sense. When I have four threads open in my head and one of them snaps into place because of another.

That isn't a productivity hack. It's just how my brain works. Some people really do need the silent room. A lot more people have been told they need it and have organized their whole work life around a virtue they don't actually possess.

So I stopped optimizing for focus. I optimize for interesting collisions instead. More tabs than the productivity books allow. Slack on when I'm not on something time-sensitive. Meetings that bleed into other meetings.

I'm getting more done and enjoying it more. Take that for what it's worth.

## Post 3 — Write the summary nobody asked for

I used to think the most valuable skill in my career would be writing code. Then I thought it was presenting to executives. Then strategy.

It's none of those. The most valuable skill, by a margin that keeps widening, is being the person who writes the summary nobody asked for.

Meeting ends without a clear decision? Send a three-line recap to the group with what you think was decided. Slack thread spirals into nothing? Post a summary and ask if you got it right. Project kicks off without an owner? Write down who's doing what and email it around.

About half the time people correct you, and that's the whole point — now you're aligned without another meeting. The other half, they accept your version because nobody else is willing to commit to one in writing. Either way, your version of events quietly becomes the version of events.

It isn't a power move. It's closer to a public service. Most teams I've worked on are drowning in ambiguity that nobody wants to be the first to name. Writing the summary is just refusing to let that ambiguity sit.

The cost is small. You'll occasionally be wrong, in writing, in front of everyone. The bigger reward is that over years you become trusted in a way that has very little to do with your title.

I'm still surprised how few people do this. It costs almost nothing. It compounds for years.
